# ASSUMPTIONS: What We Think We Know (But Haven't Proven)

**Critical Document**: This file prevents hypotheses from becoming facts.

Every assumption is labeled with confidence and validation method. None should be treated as established truth until James validates them in real conversations.

## ASSUMPTION 1: Owner Named in Reviews = Owner Involvement

**Assumption**: 
When customers mention the owner by name (Hannah, Emma, Daisy, etc.), it indicates the owner is personally involved in operations.

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- 20 of 38 evidence points (53%) include owner names
- Names appear in context of specific service delivery (design, coordination, customer interaction)
- Consistent pattern: "Hannah helped," "Emma designed," "Daisy coordinated"
- Higher-potential businesses show 3+ name mentions (Northern Flower, Flower Lounge)

**Evidence Against It**:
- Could be marketing (customer remembers name from website/initial contact, not actual involvement)
- Could be service excellence (good staff gets remembered, not owner specifically)
- Name mention could be one-time interaction, not systematic involvement
- No counter-evidence in reviews (reviews don't say "spoke to generic team")

**Confidence Level**: MEDIUM-HIGH
- Pattern is consistent
- But based only on review mentions, not operational observation
- Sample size: 20 businesses, mostly positive reviews

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "Which parts of the business still require your involvement every single day?"
- Owner lists: design, consultations, custom requests, client meetings
- Owner confirms: "Yes, I'm involved in almost everything"
- Validates: Owner IS a bottleneck

**What Would Falsify It**:
- Owner says: "I hired a design team that handles 80% of consultations"
- Owner says: "My staff handles all customer interactions"
- Owner says: "I'm mostly administrative now"
- Reveals: Name mentions don't mean personal involvement

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, not fact

## ASSUMPTION 2: Wedding Work = Operational Complexity

**Assumption**: 
Businesses that do wedding flowers experience higher operational pressure due to multi-component orders (bouquets, bridesmaids, buttonholes, arches, table arrangements).

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- 6 of 38 evidence points (16%) mention wedding work with multiple components
- Reviews explicitly mention: "bridal bouquets + bridesmaids + buttonholes + table flowers"
- All wedding mentions paired with owner involvement
- High-potential businesses (Northern Flower, Flower Lounge) both do weddings
- Wedding season = predictable surge (different from random last-minute requests)

**Evidence Against It**:
- Some low-potential businesses also do weddings (Clocktower Florist mentioned "weddings")
- Reviews don't mention complexity as a problem (customers are satisfied)
- Could be that weddings are actually profitable and easy to manage
- No review mentions "wedding coordination is hard" or "weddings are stressful"

**Confidence Level**: MEDIUM
- Pattern exists in high-potential businesses
- But wedding mention ≠ wedding complexity (could be smooth operation)
- Only 6 data points

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "Tell me about your wedding orders. How many components does a typical wedding involve?"
- Owner lists: 5-10 different deliverables, multiple meetings, custom designs
- Owner confirms: "Weddings take 3-4 months of coordination"
- Owner says: "If I had two weddings simultaneously, something would slip"
- Validates: Weddings ARE complex

**What Would Falsify It**:
- Owner says: "Weddings are our easiest business, very standardized"
- Owner says: "I handle 10 weddings per month with no stress"
- Owner says: "My system handles weddings automatically"
- Reveals: Wedding mention ≠ complexity pressure

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, not fact

## ASSUMPTION 3: Seasonal Peaks = Operational Strain

**Assumption**: 
Businesses that mention seasonal peaks (Valentine's Day, Mother's Day, Christmas, Easter) experience operational strain during those periods.

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- 7 of 38 evidence points (18%) mention seasonal occasions
- All mentions in context of successful execution ("delivered on time," "handled beautifully")
- Logical inference: If peaks are easy, wouldn't need special effort
- Predictable surge (different from random demand)

**Evidence Against It**:
- Reviews never say "seasonal peaks are stressful" or "we can't handle Mother's Day"
- All seasonal mentions are positive ("handled great despite busy time")
- Could mean: Owner has mastered peak management, not that peaks are hard
- No complaints about seasonal pressure anywhere

**Confidence Level**: LOW-MEDIUM
- Logical assumption, but minimal explicit evidence
- Only 7 data points
- All evidence shows peaks being handled well, not being stressful
- Inference (peaks require effort) ≠ confirmation (peaks are hard)

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "What becomes hardest to keep consistent when Mother's Day volume hits?"
- Owner admits: "Delivery timelines slip," "Communication breaks down," "Quality suffers"
- Owner reveals: "I have to personally work 60-hour weeks"
- Validates: Peaks ARE operationally straining

**What Would Falsify It**:
- Owner says: "Mother's Day is our easiest season, we've got it down to a system"
- Owner says: "We actually enjoy the busy periods"
- Owner says: "I have enough team capacity for any volume"
- Reveals: Seasonal peaks may not be pressure

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, not fact

## ASSUMPTION 4: Manual Coordination = Bottleneck

**Assumption**: 
When order coordination happens through email/phone (manual) rather than systems (automated), it creates a time bottleneck for the owner.

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- 8 of 38 evidence points (21%) mention coordination activities (email, consultation, custom planning)
- High-potential businesses show repeated coordination mentions
- Logical: Manual work = harder to scale than systematic work

**Evidence Against It**:
- No reviews say "coordination was slow" or "took too long"
- All coordination mentions are positive ("helpful," "patient," "organized")
- Could mean: Owner is excellent at manual coordination, not that it's a bottleneck
- No evidence of actual time spent or capacity limits

**Confidence Level**: LOW
- Inference only, no direct evidence
- All observable evidence shows manual coordination working well
- "Manual ≠ bottleneck" is assumption, not fact

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "Walk me through how you coordinate a typical wedding order. How many emails/calls?"
- Owner reveals: "Usually 15-20 email exchanges, 5-7 phone calls per wedding"
- Owner admits: "This is time-consuming and I do it personally"
- Owner confirms: "I can't scale this without help"
- Validates: Manual coordination IS a bottleneck

**What Would Falsify It**:
- Owner says: "I've systematized everything, it takes 3 emails per order"
- Owner says: "My team handles all coordination"
- Owner says: "I spend 30 minutes per order, we can handle unlimited volume"
- Reveals: Manual doesn't mean bottleneck

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, not fact

## ASSUMPTION 5: Standing Orders Reduce Workload

**Assumption**: 
Offering standing-order service (recurring, predictable deliveries) would reduce the owner's operational pressure by replacing unpredictable demand with scheduled work.

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- Logical: Predictable > unpredictable (easier to plan for)
- Recurring = no acquisition cost per delivery
- Reduces last-minute requests and emergency handling

**Evidence Against It**:
- Zero evidence from florist reviews
- No florist mentioned standing orders or contracts
- Might introduce different pressures (contract penalties, minimum volumes)
- Owner might prefer flexibility of current model

**Confidence Level**: VERY LOW
- Pure logical inference
- No real-world evidence
- Assumes standing orders solve their actual problem (we don't know what their actual problem is)
- Could be completely wrong about what they want

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "If you had 5-10 standing-order delivery contracts, what would change?"
- Owner responds positively: "That would eliminate my biggest headache"
- Owner explains problem it solves
- Owner discusses willingness to commit to recurring
- Validates: Standing orders address their actual pain

**What Would Falsify It**:
- Owner says: "We don't want recurring commitments"
- Owner says: "Our model works because it's flexible"
- Owner says: "Contracts would cost us money"
- Reveals: Standing orders are not the solution

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, HIGHLY SPECULATIVE

**CRITICAL**: This is the riskiest assumption. We've assumed standing orders are valuable without ANY evidence that businesses want them.

## ASSUMPTION 6: 100+ Reviews = Predictable Demand

**Assumption**: 
Businesses with 100+ Google reviews have established, recurring customer base = demand is more predictable.

**Evidence Supporting It**:
- Logical: High review count = many transactions = established market
- Mathematics: 100 reviews from 500+ customers (reviews are fraction of transactions)
- Consistency: High-review businesses haven't disappeared

**Evidence Against It**:
- "Recurring customer" is assumption, not fact
- Could be one-time event orders (weddings, funerals) with different customers each time
- High volume doesn't mean predictable if each order is unique
- Could mean chaotic, unpredictable demand that just works

**Confidence Level**: MEDIUM
- Logic is sound
- But unvalidated assumption about customer patterns

**How It Would Be Validated**:
James asks: "What percentage of your orders are repeat customers vs. new customers?"
- Owner says: "60% are repeat, 40% are new"
- Validates: Recurring customer base exists
- OR Owner says: "95% are new customers, each one is unique"
- Falsifies: No predictability benefit

**Current Status**: Hypothesis, not fact

## PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT

This document exists to prevent slow assumption drift.

Over time, repeated mention of "owner involvement" can make it feel like a fact. It's not. It's a hypothesis worth testing.

The system is designed to generate better conversations, not produce accurate-looking conclusions.

Every assumption here should be validated or falsified by James in Phase 2.

If an assumption is validated → update it with James' confirmation
If an assumption is falsified → remove it and learn something new
If an assumption remains untested → keep it in this document

This is how we stay honest about what we know vs. what we're guessing.
