# THE PRESSURE FRAMEWORK

**Document Purpose**: Define the complete framework for identifying business pressure and standing-order opportunities

---

## FRAMEWORK OVERVIEW

### The Three Tiers

**Tier 1: Pressure Signals**
What creates operational load that's manageable but consuming resources.

**Tier 2: Constraint Signals**
What limits the business's ability to scale beyond current pressure.

**Tier 3: Failure Signals**
When pressure breaks into actual failures (lagging indicators).

### Why This Order Matters

**Tier 1 → Opportunity**: Business is under pressure, ready to talk about solutions BEFORE pain
**Tier 2 → Window**: Business has constraints limiting growth, solution fits their reality
**Tier 3 → Too Late**: Business already experiencing failure, may have lost trust in solutions

**Best moment to sell**: Tier 1 + Tier 2 = Business recognizes they have a problem, has capacity to solve it, before it breaks.

---

## TIER 1: PRESSURE SIGNALS

These are operational loads that successful businesses carry. They're visible in reviews through mentions of complexity being managed well.

### 1.1 Seasonal Peak Handling

**Signal Pattern**: Seasonal occasions mentioned in context of successful delivery

**Examples**:
- "I ordered a Christmas bouquet for my wife over the weekend and they were delivered on time..."
- "For Valentine's day I ordered a large bouquet in plenty of time and it didn't disappoint"
- "Laura made me the most beautiful Christmas wreath for my Dad's grave"

**Why It's Pressure**:
- Every year, same surge happens
- Predictable but resource-intensive
- Next year will create same pressure
- Business handles it, but effort is real

**Validation Question**: 
"What becomes hardest to keep consistent when Mother's Day volume hits?"

**Opportunity**: 
Standing orders for seasonal peaks = predictable load + revenue predictability

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ seasonal mentions = HIGH
- 2 seasonal mentions = MEDIUM
- 1 seasonal mention = LOW

---

### 1.2 Wedding Complexity & Coordination

**Signal Pattern**: Multiple order components mentioned (bouquets, bridesmaids, buttonholes, arches, tables, arrangements)

**Examples**:
- "I ordered my bridal party bouquets, flower crowns, and buttonholes and I couldn't be happier. They arrived in these lovely personalised bags..."
- "Emma was the mastermind behind our wedding flowers (bridal and bridesmaids bouquet), buttons holes and also some floristry flair on our cake"
- "Laura provided us with the most incredible wedding flowers. With table garlands, an arch, a bouquet and buttonholes it wasn't a small order!"

**Why It's Pressure**:
- Single wedding = 3+ months of engagement
- Multiple delivery points (ceremony, reception, other locations)
- Custom design for each bridesmaid (different dress colors)
- Owner personally involved (named by customer)
- Can't standardize (each wedding is unique)

**Validation Question**: 
"If you doubled wedding bookings next year, which parts would you need to outsource?"

**Opportunity**: 
Wedding-season standing orders + delivery service = owns the customer for 3 months, recurring revenue

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ reviews mentioning multi-component weddings = HIGH
- 2 reviews with clear wedding complexity = MEDIUM
- 1 wedding mention = LOW

---

### 1.3 Last-Minute/Emergency Request Capability

**Signal Pattern**: Mentions of rush delivery, same-day service, or emergency requests being fulfilled successfully

**Examples**:
- "Last minute should be my middle name! Natalie amazed me with her speedy response..."
- "Rang up for a last minute order, was very helpful and friendly over the phone, delivered promptly"
- "The team at the flower lounge is super friendly as well. Quick to answer the phone and any questions"

**Why It's Pressure**:
- Owner personally handles urgent requests (can't systemize emergency handling)
- Owner's time consumed by urgency
- Unpredictable workload (can't batch, can't plan)
- Owner celebrating capability but exhausted by it

**Validation Question**: 
"What do you wish people would stop asking you to do in less than 24 hours?"

**Opportunity**: 
Recurring standing orders = replace unpredictable emergencies with predictable schedule

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ rush request mentions = MEDIUM (capability proven but stressful)
- 2+ rush mentions = MEDIUM
- 1 rush mention = LOW

---

### 1.4 High Customer Throughput

**Signal Pattern**: High review volume (100+ reviews)

**Why It's Pressure**:
- Business is growing/at scale
- Order volume exceeds what small team can easily manage
- Owner likely involved in most orders (scaling bottleneck)
- Demand exists, but supply is limited by owner capacity

**Validation Question**: 
"At your current throughput, where are you closest to turning orders away?"

**Opportunity**: 
High-volume business = standing orders have recurring value

**Confidence Levels**:
- 100+ reviews = HIGH
- 50-99 reviews = MEDIUM
- <50 reviews = LOW (insufficient sample to confirm scale)

---

### 1.5 Speed/Responsiveness Expectations

**Signal Pattern**: Customers specifically mention quick response times, fast turnaround, immediate action

**Examples**:
- "Quick to answer the phone and any questions"
- "Speedy response and amazing customer service"
- "Flowers arrived within hours"

**Why It's Pressure**:
- Owner is bottleneck for responsiveness (personally answers calls)
- Creates expectation of owner availability
- Owner time consumed by communication management
- Can't scale without removing owner from communication loop

**Validation Question**: 
"How much of your day gets consumed by responding to customer inquiries?"

**Opportunity**: 
Recurring orders = predictable communication schedule, batch customer contact

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ mentions of speed/responsiveness = MEDIUM
- 2 mentions = MEDIUM
- 1 mention = LOW

---

## TIER 2: CONSTRAINT SIGNALS

These are what prevents the business from easily scaling. When combined with Tier 1 pressure, they create opportunity.

### 2.1 Owner-Centric Operations

**Signal Pattern**: Owner's name mentioned specifically in multiple reviews; customers remember and reference the owner, not a generic team

**Examples**:
- "Hannah was the mastermind"
- "Thank you to Daisy and the team"
- "Emma took creative control"
- "Sian even managed to create"
- "Natalie amazed me"

**Why It's a Constraint**:
- Business cannot scale without owner
- Every design decision goes through owner
- Every customer interaction goes through owner
- Owner is the brand
- Customers choose business because of owner

**Validation Question**: 
"Which parts of the business still require your involvement every single day?"

**This Reveals**: 
What can't be delegated, what's the real constraint

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ name mentions = HIGH
- 2 name mentions + consistent context = HIGH
- 1 name mention = MEDIUM

---

### 2.2 Manual Coordination Process

**Signal Pattern**: Mentions of email back-and-forth, consultations, discussions, custom coordination

**Examples**:
- "Daisy was lovely and helpful through email throughout"
- "The consultations were fun and well organised"
- "She was also so calm and flexible with a few last minute changes"
- "Walk me through how you coordinate a typical order"

**Why It's a Constraint**:
- No workflow systems (it's all email/phone)
- Each order requires personal coordination
- Can't batch or automate
- Time-consuming process that doesn't scale

**Validation Question**: 
"Walk me through how you coordinate a typical wedding order from booking to delivery. How many emails/calls does that take?"

**This Reveals**: 
Exactly where time is spent, how much effort per order

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ coordination mentions = HIGH
- 2 coordination mentions = MEDIUM
- 1 mention = LOW

---

### 2.3 Creative Problem-Solving / Workarounds

**Signal Pattern**: Mentions of owner creating custom solutions, solving problems non-standardly, being flexible with requests

**Examples**:
- "When I asked for flowers that were out of season Sian even managed to create a very similar version"
- "Emma took creative control"
- "Hannah managed to achieve the maximum impact"
- "She figured out exactly what we needed"

**Why It's a Constraint**:
- No standardized process for common problems
- Each problem solved individually by owner
- Solution depends on owner's creativity/knowledge
- Can't scale without documenting solutions

**Validation Question**: 
"How often do you have to say 'no' to a customer request because it doesn't fit your standard process?"

**This Reveals**: 
Opportunity for processing/systematizing common requests

**Confidence Levels**:
- 3+ workaround mentions = MEDIUM
- 2 workarounds = MEDIUM
- 1 workaround = LOW

---

### 2.4 Small/Limited Team

**Signal Pattern**: Owner mentioned doing everything, team mentioned as small, or limited staff references

**Why It's a Constraint**:
- Even with team, owner still bottleneck
- Team can't grow (can't train fast enough)
- All decisions flow through owner
- Can't parallelize work

**Validation Question**: 
"If you hired two more people tomorrow, how would you use them?"

**This Reveals**: 
Whether owner can delegate, what's holdable vs. owner-specific

**Confidence Levels**:
- Consistent pattern of owner "doing everything" = HIGH
- Limited team mentions = MEDIUM

---

## TIER 3: FAILURE SIGNALS

These appear AFTER pressure becomes too much. They're lagging indicators. Useful for understanding existing failures, but by then customer trust is damaged.

### 3.1 Delivery Failures

**Examples**:
- "Delivery was late"
- "Never arrived"
- "No driver in the delivery location"

---

### 3.2 Quality Issues

**Examples**:
- "Flowers drooped within days"
- "Reduced quality"
- "Didn't live up to expectations"

---

### 3.3 Communication Breakdowns

**Examples**:
- "Not informed of quality issues"
- "Lack of empathy from staff"
- "Poor response to concerns"

---

### 3.4 Availability/Capacity Failure

**Examples**:
- "Couldn't get a rush order fulfilled"
- "Booking capacity full"
- "Turned away last-minute request"

---

## WHY TIER 1 + 2 > TIER 3

**Timing of Intervention**:

```
Tier 1 Pressure Building
    ↓ (Business handling it but exhausted)
Tier 2 Constraints Limiting Scaling
    ↓ (Owner is bottleneck, can't grow)
Tier 3 Failures Beginning
    ↓ (Something breaks)
Total System Collapse
```

**Opportunity Windows**:
- At Tier 1+2: Owner recognizes problem, ready to solve it, high trust (business is successful)
- At Tier 3: Owner frustrated, may have given up, trust in solutions is lower
- After collapse: Business is gone or recovering, opportunity missed

**Standing Order Opportunity**:
- Best at Tier 1+2: "I'm under pressure and need help"
- Harder at Tier 3: "I'm broken and need to fix it" (defensiveness, loss of trust)

## CONFIDENCE ASSESSMENT RULES

### How to Assign Confidence

1. **Count independent mentions** (same idea mentioned in different reviews)
   - 3+ mentions = HIGH confidence
   - 2 mentions = MEDIUM confidence
   - 1 mention = LOW confidence

2. **Check consistency** of pattern
   - Same owner name in 3 reviews = HIGH
   - Owner handling each wedding = HIGH
   - Single manager in charge = MEDIUM

3. **Look at context quality**
   - Clear, explicit mentions > vague references
   - Named persons > "staff"
   - Specific examples > general statements

### Confidence Is NOT a Score

It's a statement about evidence quality:
- HIGH = We have strong evidence, multiple independent mentions, consistent pattern
- MEDIUM = We have some evidence, pattern is emerging, needs validation
- LOW = Single mention or unclear context, need more data

No "73%" or "85%". Just honest assessment of what we know from the evidence.

## APPLICATION TO OTHER NICHES

**This framework is designed for service businesses.**

**High confidence transfer to**:
- Restaurants (seasonal peaks, event catering, delivery complexity)
- Legal services (project complexity, seasonal surges, manual coordination)
- Contractors (project coordination, emergency calls, owner dependency)
- Consultancies (consulting projects, seasonal demand, owner-centric)

**Moderate confidence transfer to**:
- Retail (seasonal peaks exist but different, less owner dependency)
- Manufacturing (less last-minute demand, more planning-based)

**Low confidence transfer to**:
- Software (very different operational structure)
- Online services (no delivery, different constraints)

---

## RULES FOR FRAMEWORK APPLICATION

1. **Evidence First**: Never hypothesize without evidence from reviews
2. **Confidence Matters**: Don't flag low-confidence signals
3. **Context Is King**: One mention of "delivery" isn't a signal; three mentions with consistent context is
4. **Tier Order Matters**: Look for Tier 1+2, not Tier 3
5. **Conversation > Score**: Goal is a good question, not a numerical ranking
6. **Human Judgment Final**: James' conversation validates or refutes hypothesis
